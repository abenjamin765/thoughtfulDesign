## The situation

A team building a healthcare appointment portal is convinced their new booking flow is better. "Let's validate it," someone says. They schedule five sessions, show the prototype, and ask each patient, "Do you like this? Would you use it?" Everyone nods politely. The team ships, confident.

Booking abandonment does not improve.

## The hidden problem

The team ran "research" but learned nothing, because they never named what they were trying to find out.

| What they did | What was missing |
| --- | --- |
| Showed a prototype and asked "Do you like it?" | A clear decision the research would inform |
| Collected polite agreement | A belief that could actually be proven wrong |
| Felt validated | Any evidence about *why* patients abandon booking |

They were not testing a risk. They were collecting reassurance. The questions were leading, the sessions confirmed what they hoped, and the real problem — why stressed patients drop out mid-booking — went unexamined.

> They asked, "Do users like our solution?"
> They needed to ask, "What is actually making patients give up?"

## Research as sensemaking

Research is not a ceremony you perform to approve a design. It is how a team reduces uncertainty about reality so it can decide well. The shift is from *validation* (seeking a yes) to *sensemaking* (learning what the situation is really asking of you).

That shift starts with three things, before any session is booked:

- **A decision** the research will inform.
- **A risky assumption** — a belief that could be wrong and that matters.
- **A falsification condition** — what you would have to see to change your mind.

<Callout type="tip">

If you cannot say what result would make you abandon your current design, you are not doing research — you are looking for applause. Name the result that would change your mind before you start.

</Callout>
