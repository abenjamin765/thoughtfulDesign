## The situation

The healthcare team ran five sessions and has pages of notes: pauses, sighs, quotes, wrong turns. The danger now is the most common one in research — turning a pile of observations into whatever conclusion the team already wanted.

## From observation to insight

Synthesis is the work of moving up a ladder:

1. **Observation** — what literally happened ("Patient 3 paused on the insurance screen and left").
2. **Pattern** — what repeats across people ("4 of 5 stalled when asked for insurance details").
3. **Insight** — what it means for the design ("Patients abandon when asked for information they don't have on hand").
4. **Decision** — what to do ("Let patients confirm the appointment first and add insurance afterward").

Raw observations are not findings. Findings are not insights until they generalize a pattern. And insights are not useful until they connect to a decision.

> A report full of observations informs no one.
> An insight tied to a decision changes the product.

## Guard against bias

Synthesis is where wishful thinking sneaks back in. The team that wanted a "faster flow" will find quotes about speed and ignore the four patients who stalled because of missing information.

Protect against it:

- Look hardest at evidence that **contradicts** your hunch.
- Count patterns across participants instead of leaning on one vivid quote.
- Keep the **strength** of each piece of evidence visible (observed beats stated).

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is "synthesis as highlight reel" — assembling the quotes that support a decision already made. If your synthesis never surprises you or changes your mind about anything, you probably skipped the thinking.

</Callout>

## Try it

Use the **Assumption Audit** resource. Take a real assumption from your work and fill in: what you believe, why, the evidence you have (and its strength), what you would need to learn, and the decision the research would inform. Then, if you have any research notes, practice the observation → pattern → insight → decision ladder on one finding.

<Reveal label="What good looks like">

A strong synthesis names a pattern across multiple participants, states an insight in plain language, links it to a specific design decision, and is honest about evidence strength and contradicting cases. For the portal: "4 of 5 patients abandoned at the insurance step because they didn't have details on hand (observed). Insight: booking should not require information patients lack in the moment. Decision: split insurance out of the booking flow. Caveat: 1 patient had details ready and breezed through — so make the later step easy to find."

</Reveal>

## Summary

- Research is sensemaking, not a hunt for approval. Start from a decision, a risky assumption, and what would change your mind.
- Separate research questions (for the team) from interview questions (for participants); avoid leading questions.
- Rank assumptions by impact-if-wrong and current evidence; test the riskiest first.
- Weigh evidence by strength: observed beats reported beats stated.
- Match the method to the kind of uncertainty; pair qualitative ("why") with quantitative ("how much").
- Synthesize up the ladder — observation, pattern, insight, decision — while guarding against confirmation bias.
- This module lives in the **Understand** and **Test** steps of the loop.

**Key terms:** research question, interview question, assumption, evidence strength, qualitative vs. quantitative, synthesis, confirmation bias, research ethics.
