## The situation

The healthcare team restarts. This time, before booking sessions, they separate three things they had been blurring together: research questions, assumptions, and evidence.

## Research questions vs. interview questions

These are not the same, and confusing them ruins studies.

| Research question (for the team) | Interview question (for the participant) |
| --- | --- |
| Why do patients abandon booking before confirming? | "Walk me through the last time you booked an appointment." |
| What makes patients distrust the appointment times shown? | "What were you thinking when you saw these times?" |

A research question is what the *team* needs to learn. An interview question is what you actually *say* to a participant — and it must not lead them to an answer.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is asking participants to design or predict: "Would you use this?" or "What would make this better?" People are kind and imaginative, so they say yes and invent features. Ask about past behavior and real experiences instead.

</Callout>

## Assumptions and risk

Every design rests on assumptions. The job is not to eliminate them — it is to find the *riskiest* ones and test those first.

Rank an assumption by two things:

- **Impact if wrong:** how badly does the design break?
- **Current evidence:** how much do we actually know?

The assumptions that are high-impact and low-evidence are where research pays off. In the portal: "patients abandon because the flow is too long" is high-impact — and the team has no real evidence for it. That is the one to test.

## Evidence strength

Not all findings carry the same weight. A rough hierarchy:

1. **Observed behavior** (you watched it happen) — strongest.
2. **Reported behavior** (they told you what they did) — medium.
3. **Stated preference / prediction** (they told you what they want or would do) — weakest.

> "Users said they want it faster" is a hunch wearing a lab coat.
> "Three of five patients abandoned at the insurance step" is evidence.

<Callout type="tip">

When you read a finding, ask: did someone *observe* this, *hear* it, or *guess* it? Label the strength. Decisions built on stated preferences need more support before you trust them.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="A quick assumption test">

For any belief driving your design, complete: "We believe ___. We're confident because ___. We'd be wrong if we saw ___." If the middle blank is empty or the last blank is unimaginable, you have found an assumption worth researching.

</Reveal>
