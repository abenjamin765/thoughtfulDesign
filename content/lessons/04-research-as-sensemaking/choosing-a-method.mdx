## The situation

The healthcare team now has a sharp research question — *why do patients abandon booking before confirming?* — and a guess that the insurance step is the culprit. The question is what to actually do. Interview people? Run a survey? Look at the data?

## Match the method to the uncertainty

Method choice follows the *kind* of question you have.

| If you need to know… | Reach for… | It tells you… |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Why something happens | Interviews, usability testing, contextual inquiry | Reasoning, motivation, friction |
| How many / how often | Analytics, funnels, surveys at scale | Scale and frequency |
| Whether A beats B on a metric | A/B test | Relative performance |
| What the real context is | Contextual inquiry (observe in situ) | Environment, constraints, workarounds |

For the portal, the team pairs two methods: **funnel analytics** to confirm *where* abandonment happens (it spikes at the insurance step), then **moderated usability sessions** to learn *why* (patients do not have their insurance details on hand when they book).

## Qualitative and quantitative are partners

Quantitative methods tell you *what* and *how much*. Qualitative methods tell you *why*. The portal team needed both: the numbers located the problem; the sessions explained it. Neither alone would have led to the fix — letting patients book first and add insurance later.

<Callout type="tip">

Start from the decision and the assumption, then pick the smallest method that could change your mind. A focused five-person usability study often beats a giant survey that answers the wrong question.

</Callout>

## Research ethics, briefly

Especially in healthcare, research touches vulnerable people. Get informed consent, protect privacy, never deceive, and let participants stop at any time. Ethical research is also better research — people tell you the truth when they trust you.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is choosing the method you are most comfortable with rather than the one the question needs. A team that loves interviews will interview its way through a "how many" question and still not know the scale.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="What good looks like">

A strong method choice names the decision, the assumption, the method, and why that method fits — and it is as small as it can be. For example: "To decide whether to split insurance out of booking, we'll watch 5 patients book (why) after confirming via funnel data that the insurance step is the top drop-off (how much)."

</Reveal>
