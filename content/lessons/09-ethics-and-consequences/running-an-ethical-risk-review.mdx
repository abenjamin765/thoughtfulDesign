## The situation

The marketplace team agrees the pre-checked plan crossed a line. They want a way to catch these problems *before* shipping, not after refund requests spike. The tool is an ethical risk review — a short, structured check you can run on any feature.

## The review

Walk a proposed design through these questions:

| Question | For the protection plan |
| --- | --- |
| Intended outcome | Increase revenue per checkout |
| Affected users | All buyers, especially rushed and first-time ones |
| Possible harms | Paying for something not chosen; lost trust |
| Power imbalance | Marketplace controls defaults and pricing; buyer sees a clock |
| Missing transparency | True cost and the default itself are easy to miss |
| Safer alternative | Unchecked box, honest one-line case, easy accept |

The review does not require canceling features. It requires naming the harm honestly and finding a design that keeps the legitimate value while reducing the cost to users.

## From review to redesign

Almost every ethical problem has a better-designed alternative that a team can live with. The pre-checked plan becomes an honest opt-in. Hidden fees become an upfront total. A buried cancel link becomes a clear one. In most cases the business keeps the real value and loses only the part that depended on users not noticing.

> Ethics in product work is rarely about grand refusals.
> It is mostly about choosing the honest version of a feature you were going to build anyway.

## Try it

Use the **Ethical Risk Review** resource. Take a real feature and answer the six questions: intended outcome, affected users, possible harms, power imbalance, missing transparency, and safer alternative. End with one concrete redesign that keeps the value and reduces the harm.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is using the review to *justify* a decision already made ("we considered ethics, so it's fine"). The review only matters if it can change the design. If yours never produces a safer alternative, you are rubber-stamping, not reviewing.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="What good looks like">

A strong review names a specific, plausible harm and a specific person it lands on, acknowledges the power asymmetry honestly, and ends with a redesign the business can accept. For the marketplace: "Harm: rushed first-time buyers pay for an unchosen plan and feel tricked, raising refunds and eroding trust. Redesign: unchecked opt-in with an honest one-line case and one-tap accept. We keep most of the revenue and protect repeat purchase."

</Reveal>

## Summary

- Ethics asks what a design makes easier, harder, normal, hidden, rewarded, or risky — and for whom.
- Ethical problems usually look defensible and incremental, not villainous.
- Persuasion respects the user's ability to choose; manipulation undermines it.
- Dark patterns are manipulation made reusable; power asymmetry raises the stakes of every default.
- An ethical risk review (outcome, users, harms, power, transparency, alternative) turns concern into a safer design.
- Most ethical fixes keep the legitimate value and drop only the part that relied on users not noticing.
- This module is the **Account** step of the loop.

**Key terms:** design ethics, persuasion vs. manipulation, dark pattern, power asymmetry, transparency, consent, ethical risk review.
