## The situation

A marketplace growth team needs to lift revenue per checkout. They pre-check an optional paid "purchase protection plan" so it is added by default. Conversion to the plan jumps. The metric turns green. The change is fully legal, and leadership is pleased.

Then refund requests climb, support fills with "I never meant to buy this," and trust scores dip among repeat buyers.

## The hidden problem

The win was real, and so was the cost — it just landed somewhere the dashboard did not show.

| Who benefits | Who bears the cost |
| --- | --- |
| The business (short-term revenue) | Buyers who pay for something they didn't choose |
| The growth metric | Support, who field the complaints |
| This quarter | Long-term trust and repeat purchase |

This is not cartoon villainy. No one set out to deceive. The team optimized a metric and let a default quietly do the rest. That is exactly how most ethical problems in products actually happen — defensibly, incrementally, with good intentions.

> The design did not lie to anyone.
> It just made the company's preferred choice the easy one and the user's real choice the effortful one.

## The ethical lens

Design ethics is not a separate philosophy seminar. It is a practical question you ask of any design: **what does this make easier, harder, normal, hidden, rewarded, or risky — and for whom?**

A pre-checked box makes spending easier and noticing harder. It makes an opt-out the user's burden. It rewards inattention. Naming those effects plainly is the first ethical skill — and it usually reveals the problem without any moralizing.

<Callout type="tip">

When a change lifts a metric, ask who pays for the lift. If the answer is "users who don't notice," you have found an ethical risk, regardless of whether it is legal.

</Callout>
