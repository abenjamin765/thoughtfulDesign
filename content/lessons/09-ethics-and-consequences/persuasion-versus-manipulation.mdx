## The situation

The marketplace team defends the pre-checked plan: "Every product nudges users. Recommending a protection plan is just persuasion." They are half right — persuasion is legitimate. The question is whether this crosses into manipulation.

## The line

Persuasion and manipulation can look similar on the surface. The difference is what they do to the user's ability to choose.

| Persuasion | Manipulation |
| --- | --- |
| Gives honest information to help a choice | Hides or distorts information to force a choice |
| Works even if the user is paying attention | Works *because* the user is not paying attention |
| The user could reasonably say no | Saying no is made hidden, hard, or costly |
| Respects the user's goals | Exploits the user's inattention or emotion |

A clear recommendation with honest pricing is persuasion. A pre-checked box that relies on you not noticing is manipulation. Same feature, different relationship to the user's autonomy.

## Dark patterns and power asymmetry

**Dark patterns** are designs that manipulate users into actions against their interest: hidden costs, sneaky defaults, buried cancel links, fake urgency, confusing opt-outs. They are manipulation made into reusable templates.

What makes them especially serious is **power asymmetry**: the product holds far more information and control than the user. The marketplace knows its pricing logic, its defaults, and its data; the buyer sees a checkout and a clock. When one side holds that much more power, exploiting the gap is not clever — it is a breach of trust.

> Every default is a small exercise of power.
> Ethics is using that power in the user's interest, not only the business's.

<Callout type="tip">

A quick test: would the design still work if you explained it out loud to the user? "We pre-checked this so you'd be more likely to miss it" fails the test. "We recommend this plan and here's why" passes.

</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is assuming intent settles the question. No one needs to *intend* harm for a dark pattern to harm. Judge the design by its effect on the user's ability to choose, not by whether the team meant well.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="Persuasion done right">

The ethical version of the marketplace plan: leave the box unchecked, show a one-line honest case for the plan ("covers damage in transit; ~5% of orders claim it"), and make accepting it one easy tap. Revenue may be lower than the pre-checked version, but it is revenue from real choices — and it does not erode the trust the marketplace depends on.

</Reveal>
