## The situation

The dashboard designer now asks the strategy question directly. To choose among her three alert directions, she maps the forces around the decision: the goals, the constraints, and the tradeoffs each option represents.

## Three forces to make explicit

**Goals.** Separate the *user need* from the *business goal*, because they often pull in different directions.

- User need: ops staff need to catch real incidents quickly without drowning in noise.
- Business goal: reduce mean time to resolution, which leadership tracks.

Both are legitimate. Thoughtful design serves both honestly instead of pretending only one exists.

**Constraints.** The real limits the decision lives within: three weeks to ship, no backend changes, an ops team already fatigued by notifications. Constraints are not failures of ambition — they are the shape of the actual problem.

**Tradeoffs.** What each option gives up. Aggressive alerts buy speed at the cost of calm. Configuration buys flexibility at the cost of simplicity most users will never use.

## Choosing with judgment

With the forces visible, the choice becomes reasoning instead of agonizing. Given a fatigued ops team (constraint), a need to catch real incidents fast (user need), and a metric for resolution time (business goal), the designer chooses **smart prioritized alerts** — urgent items surface, the rest stay quiet — with simple defaults and configuration deferred to later.

She did not find a perfect option. She found the strongest option achievable under real constraints. That is **satisficing**, and it is what real decisions require.

> Perfect is a luxury of unlimited time and information.
> Judgment is what you use when you have neither.

<Callout type="tip">

When goals conflict, do not average them into a compromise that serves none. Decide which leads, design primarily for it, and protect the others where you can.

</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is treating constraints as enemies to overcome. Often the constraint is the most honest description of the problem. "Ship in three weeks for a fatigued team" is not in the way of the design — it *is* the design brief.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="A decision-forces sketch">

For any hard choice, write four lines: top user need, top business goal, the binding constraint, and the key tradeoff. If the right option is still unclear, you are usually missing information about one of these four — go get it.

</Reveal>
