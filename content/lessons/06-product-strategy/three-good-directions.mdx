## The situation

A designer on the enterprise dashboard team has three solid directions for a new "alerts" feature. One surfaces alerts aggressively to drive fast response. One keeps them quiet to avoid overwhelming users. One makes them fully configurable. Each is well-crafted. Each serves a different goal. She has been staring at them for two days, unable to choose.

## The hidden problem

The paralysis is not about the designs. It is about an unmade decision underneath them: *which goal matters most right now?*

| Direction | Goal it serves | What it sacrifices |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Aggressive alerts | Fast incident response | Calm, focus, signal-to-noise |
| Quiet alerts | Low cognitive load | Speed when something is urgent |
| Fully configurable | Flexibility | Simplicity; most users never configure |

You cannot choose between directions until you know what the product is trying to achieve. Three good options are not a design problem. They are a strategy problem wearing a design costume.

> She did not have three designs to choose from.
> She had one unanswered question: what matters most here?

## Judgment, not taste

Design judgment is the ability to choose what matters most under real constraints. It is different from taste. Taste asks "which looks best?" Judgment asks "which best serves the goal, given what we know and what we are limited by?"

A thoughtful designer makes the goal explicit, names the tradeoffs honestly, and chooses — then can explain the choice to anyone who asks.

<Callout type="tip">

When you are stuck between good options, stop refining them. The blocker is almost never craft. Go find or force a decision about the priority goal, and the right direction usually becomes obvious.

</Callout>
