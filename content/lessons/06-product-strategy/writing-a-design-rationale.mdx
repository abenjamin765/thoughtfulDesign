## The situation

The dashboard designer has made her call: prioritized alerts with simple defaults. In the old days she would have just built it. But a thoughtful designer knows a decision is only as durable as the reasoning behind it. So she writes a short rationale — and it pays off the first time someone asks "why didn't you make it configurable?"

## What a rationale contains

A design rationale connects a recommendation to the forces behind it. A useful one is short and covers:

1. **Recommendation** — what you are proposing.
2. **User need** — the human problem it serves.
3. **Business goal** — the outcome the company wants.
4. **Evidence** — what supports the choice (and how strong it is).
5. **Tradeoffs** — what you are deliberately giving up.
6. **Risks** — what could make this wrong.
7. **Next step** — what happens now, and what would make you revisit.

> A rationale is not a justification written after the fact.
> It is the thinking made visible, so others can check it and the future can revisit it.

## Why it matters

A written rationale does three jobs. It lets stakeholders evaluate your *reasoning* rather than react to your *pixels*. It survives turnover and time, so a decision is not blindly reversed a year later. And it forces you to notice when your own reasoning is thin — if the "evidence" line is empty, you have learned something.

<Callout type="tip">

Name the strongest argument *against* your choice in the rationale, and answer it. A rationale that engages its best counterargument is far more persuasive than one that pretends none exists.

</Callout>

## Try it

Use the **Design Rationale Template** resource. Take a real or recent design decision and write a rationale covering recommendation, user need, business goal, evidence, tradeoffs, risks, and next step. Keep it to a single page.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is writing a rationale that only lists strengths. If there are no tradeoffs and no risks, it is marketing, not reasoning. Every real decision gives something up — name it.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="What good looks like">

A strong rationale fits on one page, distinguishes user need from business goal, labels its evidence by strength, states the tradeoff plainly, and names a risk with what would trigger a rethink. Example close: "Risk: if configuration requests spike in month one, we revisit. Next step: ship prioritized defaults; instrument how often users want to tune thresholds."

</Reveal>

## Summary

- Design judgment is choosing what matters most under real constraints — distinct from taste.
- Make the forces explicit: user needs vs. business goals, constraints, and tradeoffs.
- Satisfice: choose the strongest option achievable under real limits and incomplete information.
- Write a rationale connecting recommendation, need, goal, evidence, tradeoffs, risks, and next step.
- Engage the best counterargument; name what you give up.
