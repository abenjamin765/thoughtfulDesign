## The situation

The healthcare team is ready to fix the portal, but they want to do it systematically rather than patching whatever the last complaint mentioned. They run a human variation audit.

## A structured audit

For a given flow, walk through each dimension of variation and ask: who could be excluded here, and how?

| Dimension | Question | Portal example fix |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Visual | Enough contrast? Meaning beyond color? | Raise contrast; add labels to color cues |
| Motor | Targets big enough? Keyboard-operable? | Enlarge tap targets; remove hover-only actions |
| Cognitive | Plain language? Memory aids? Progress shown? | Rewrite jargon; show step progress; allow save-and-resume |
| Emotional | Calm under stress? Forgiving of mistakes? | Reassuring tone; easy undo; no punishing timeouts |
| Linguistic | Avoids idioms/acronyms? Translatable? | Simplify wording; support translation |
| Situational | Holds up in glare, one-handed, slow network? | Test in real conditions; lightweight pages |
| Assistive tech | Proper labels and structure? | Label controls; logical reading order |

The audit converts a vague goal ("make it accessible") into a concrete list of changes tied to real people.

## The curb-cut effect

Curb cuts were built for wheelchairs and now help anyone with a stroller, suitcase, or cart. Digital accessibility works the same way. Captions help in noisy waiting rooms. Plain language helps everyone who is rushed. Large targets help one-handed users. Designing for the edges improves the middle.

> Designing for variation is not charity for a few.
> It is quality for the many — including the stressed, tired version of every user.

## Try it

Use the **Human Variation Audit** resource. Take a real flow and walk it through visual, motor, cognitive, emotional, linguistic, situational, and assistive-technology lenses. For each, note one barrier and one concrete fix. Then pick the single change that would help the most people and do it first.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is auditing and then deprioritizing every fix as "nice to have." Accessibility is a measure of design quality, not a backlog of optional extras. Treat the highest-impact exclusions as defects.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="What good looks like">

A strong audit names a real person behind each barrier, proposes a specific change, and ranks by how many people it affects. For the portal: "Cognitive — jargon excludes stressed, lower-literacy patients (large group); fix: rewrite confirmation and error copy in plain language. Do this first." It reads as a prioritized plan, not a reassurance.

</Reveal>

## Summary

- Good design respects human variation: visual, motor, cognitive, auditory, linguistic, emotional, and situational.
- Variation is permanent, temporary, and situational — and the same barriers hurt all three.
- WCAG-informed thinking asks whether content is perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust. Compliance is the floor, not the goal.
- Never carry meaning by color alone; support keyboard use; write plain language.
- A human variation audit turns "make it accessible" into prioritized, concrete changes.
- Accessibility improvements help broadly — the curb-cut effect.
- This module spans **Shape** and **Account** in the loop.

**Key terms:** human variation, permanent/temporary/situational, WCAG (perceivable, operable, understandable, robust), keyboard access, plain language, curb-cut effect, human variation audit.
