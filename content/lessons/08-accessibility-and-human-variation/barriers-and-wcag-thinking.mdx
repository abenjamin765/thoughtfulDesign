## The situation

The healthcare team accepts that patients vary enormously. Now they need a way to find the specific barriers in their portal — and a framework so they are not just guessing.

## Common barriers, by kind of variation

Barriers map to the dimensions of human variation. A quick scan:

| Variation | Example barrier in the portal |
| --- | --- |
| Visual | Low contrast text; meaning signaled by color alone |
| Motor | Tiny tap targets; actions that require precise hovering |
| Cognitive | Dense jargon; long forms with no progress or memory aids |
| Auditory | Video instructions with no captions or transcript |
| Linguistic | Idioms and acronyms that assume fluency |
| Situational | Anything that fails under glare, stress, or one-handed use |

Notice how many of these are the same craft issues from Module 7 — contrast, target size, plain language — now seen through the lens of who gets excluded.

## WCAG-informed thinking

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are the standard reference. You do not need them memorized, but four ideas guide most decisions. Content should be:

- **Perceivable** — people can sense it (sufficient contrast, text alternatives for images, captions).
- **Operable** — people can use it (full keyboard access, large enough targets, no reliance on hover).
- **Understandable** — people can comprehend it (plain language, predictable behavior, clear errors).
- **Robust** — it works with assistive technology (proper labels and structure for screen readers).

> WCAG is not a box to tick at the end.
> It is a set of questions to ask while you design: can people perceive, operate, understand, and rely on this?

<Callout type="tip">

Three checks catch a large share of barriers: navigate the whole flow with only the keyboard, view it in grayscale, and read every label aloud as if you were stressed and new to the domain.

</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is treating WCAG as the ceiling — "we passed AA, we're done." Compliance is the floor. A flow can technically conform and still exhaust a stressed patient. Aim for genuine usability across variation, not just a passing audit.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="Color is a signal, not the only signal">

In the portal, "urgent" appointments were shown in red and "routine" in green — invisible to many color-blind patients and washed out by glare. The fix kept color but added a text label and an icon, so the meaning survives any viewing condition. Never let color be the sole carrier of meaning.

</Reveal>
