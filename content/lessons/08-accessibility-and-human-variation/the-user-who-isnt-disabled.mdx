## The situation

A patient needs to book a follow-up appointment. She is holding her toddler with one arm, standing on a sunny bus platform, and she is anxious about the appointment itself. She opens the healthcare portal and immediately struggles: tiny tap targets, low-contrast text washed out by glare, and dense medical jargon she has to parse while distracted.

She is not blind. She is not motor-impaired. By a narrow definition, she is not "disabled." And yet the design excludes her.

## The hidden problem

The portal was designed for an imaginary user: calm, focused, two-handed, fluent, indoors, on a fast connection. Almost no real patient matches that profile all the time.

| The assumed user | The real patient |
| --- | --- |
| Calm and focused | Anxious, distracted, multitasking |
| Two hands free | One hand on a child or a railing |
| Perfect lighting | Glare, dim rooms, cracked screens |
| Fluent in medical terms | Confused by jargon and acronyms |

Every assumption the design made is a way it can exclude someone — not just people with permanent disabilities, but anyone whose moment does not match the fantasy.

> Accessibility is not a feature for a small group of "other" users.
> It is how design holds up for real people in real situations.

## Accessibility is about human variation

The narrow view treats accessibility as compliance for people with permanent disabilities. The thoughtful view is broader: humans vary — in vision, motor control, cognition, language, emotion, and situation — and good design respects that range.

Those variations come in three kinds:

- **Permanent** — e.g. low vision, limited dexterity.
- **Temporary** — e.g. an arm in a cast, a migraine.
- **Situational** — e.g. bright sun, one hand busy, high stress.

Design for the range, and you serve far more people far more often — including the version of every user who is tired, rushed, or stressed.

<Callout type="tip">

When you picture "the user," picture them at their worst moment, not their best: distracted, in a hurry, on a bad screen. Design that holds up then holds up for everyone.

</Callout>
