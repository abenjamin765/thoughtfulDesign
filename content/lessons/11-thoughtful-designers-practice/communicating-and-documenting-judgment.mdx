## The situation

The designer's judgment is only as valuable as her ability to share it. A great decision explained as "I made it cleaner" is indistinguishable from taste. The same decision explained with its reasoning becomes something a team can trust, challenge, and build on.

## Communicating rationale

Thoughtful designers translate decisions into reasoning others can evaluate. Whether in a standup, a doc, or a review, a clear communication of a design decision names three things: **the change, the reason, and the tradeoff.**

| Weak update | Strong update |
| --- | --- |
| "Improved the AI feature." | "Moved AI suggestions behind a one-tap approve, because auto-send caused costly errors. Trades a little speed for trust and recoverability." |
| "Cleaned up onboarding." | "Reordered onboarding so value comes before the account step, because users dropped at the form. Trades immediate data capture for completion." |

The strong version invites scrutiny — which is the point. Reasoning you can defend is reasoning others can improve.

## Knowing when to push, pause, or compromise

Judgment includes reading the situation:

- **Push** for more evidence when a decision is high-impact and rests on a risky, untested assumption.
- **Pause** when the team is deciding before it understands the problem.
- **Compromise** when the remaining options are all reasonable and the cost of delay exceeds the benefit of being right.

Knowing which move fits is itself a skill — and it improves with reflection on how past calls turned out.

## Documenting decisions

Decisions that live only in memory get relitigated and silently reversed. A **decision record** is a short, durable note that captures what was decided, why, what it traded off, and what would make you revisit it. It is institutional memory and, working with PMs and engineers, a shared reference that keeps everyone aligned after the meeting ends.

> Undocumented judgment evaporates.
> Documented judgment compounds.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is documenting only the final choice and not the reasoning. A record that says "we chose prioritized alerts" without the why is nearly useless a year later, when the context that made it right has been forgotten.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="The shape of a good decision record">

Decision, date, and owner; the reasoning (user need, goal, evidence); the tradeoff accepted; the main alternative and why it lost; and the trigger that would make you reopen it. Short enough to actually write, complete enough to actually help.

</Reveal>
