## The situation

A marketplace wants more sellers, so it builds a "list your first item" flow. Every field works. Validation fires correctly. The data saves. By any technical measure, it is done.

Yet new sellers keep abandoning it halfway. The team is baffled — nothing is broken.

## The hidden problem

The flow works. It just asks too much, in the wrong order, with too little guidance.

| What the team measured | What the seller experienced |
| --- | --- |
| "All fields function" | "Why do you need my tax info before I've even described the item?" |
| "Validation works" | A red error appears only after submitting the whole form |
| "The page loads" | A blank screen with no sense of how many steps remain |

"Working" is a developer's word. "Usable" is the user's. A flow can pass every functional test and still feel like a chore — and people abandon chores.

> The form was not broken.
> It was just exhausting, and exhaustion is a design decision too.

## Craft makes thinking visible

You have spent this course learning to see systems, model objects, research people, and choose well. Interaction craft is where all of that reaches the user. If the thinking is good but the interface is effortful, the user never feels the thinking — they just feel the effort.

Craft is the discipline of turning understanding into a clear, low-effort, trustworthy experience: sensible flows, honest states, helpful feedback, and reduced load. The rest of this module is the toolkit.

<Callout type="tip">

When something "works but feels wrong," resist redesigning the visuals first. Ask where the flow demands unnecessary effort, attention, or trust. That is usually the real defect.

</Callout>
