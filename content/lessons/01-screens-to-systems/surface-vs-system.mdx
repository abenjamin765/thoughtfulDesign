## The situation

The operations dashboard from the last lesson is not unusual. Most products that feel "confusing" are confusing for reasons that do not live on the screen. To work at that depth, it helps to name the layers we design across.

## Three layers of design work

Design happens at three depths at once. None is more virtuous than another — but modern UX increasingly asks designers to work in the deeper two.

| Layer | Question it answers | Example move |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Interface design | How does this look and behave? | Improve the hierarchy of a result card |
| Product design | What should this feature do, and for whom? | Decide what "active account" means |
| Systems design | What objects, decisions, and consequences does this create? | Reconcile three teams' conflicting definitions |

The dashboard request lived at the interface layer in the ask, but its real problem lived at the product and systems layers.

## Design as decision-making

Every design choice encodes a decision about what matters. When you choose what to show first, you decide what is important. When you choose a default, you decide what most people will do. When you choose a metric to highlight, you decide what the team will optimize.

This is why "just make it simpler" is never neutral. Simplifying always moves complexity somewhere — to another screen, another team, or another moment in time.

> Simplicity on the screen is not the same as simplicity in the system.
> Sometimes a cleaner screen just hides a messier system.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is treating every request at the layer it arrived in. A surface request can have a system cause. Translating the request to the right layer is part of the work, not a detour from it.

</Callout>

## Hidden dependencies

Behind a simple interface sit dependencies the screen never shows: who owns the data, which teams are affected, what behavior a change will encourage, and what happens downstream when you move complexity around.

Hiding advanced settings makes a screen calmer and sends power users to the support queue. Adding a single "engagement" number makes a dashboard tidier and flattens very different situations into one figure people then misread.

<Reveal label="A quick way to surface dependencies">

For any change, ask three questions: Who else touches this? What behavior will it encourage? What happens one step downstream? If you cannot answer, you have found something worth investigating before you design.

</Reveal>

## Carry forward

Interface, product, and system are not competing skills — they are depths of the same craft. The thoughtful designer learns to tell which layer a problem really lives in, and to work there even when the request arrived at the surface.
