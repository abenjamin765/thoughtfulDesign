## The situation

A team building the school analytics platform jumps straight into screens. They design a "Reports page," a "Students page," and a "Classes page." Each page is built by a different person, in a different week, with a different idea of what a student record contains.

Months later, the product feels incoherent. A Student looks one way on the Reports page and another way on the Classes page. Teachers ask, "Is this the same student?" The team keeps redesigning pages, and the confusion keeps coming back.

## The hidden problem

The team designed pages before agreeing on objects. So every page reinvented the same things slightly differently.

| What they designed first | What they skipped |
| --- | --- |
| Pages (Reports, Students, Classes) | A shared definition of Student, Class, Assessment |
| Layouts and navigation | How those objects relate to each other |
| Features (filter, export) | What attributes each object actually has |

Confusion in the interface usually traces back to confusion in the model. If the team cannot say clearly what a "Student" is, no page about students will feel clear.

> The team kept redesigning the screens.
> The problem was that they never agreed on the things.

## What an object is

An object is a meaningful thing in a product's domain that users recognize and care about: a Student, a Class, an Assessment, a Score. Objects are not pages, features, or buttons. They are the nouns of the user's world.

The shift at the heart of this module is simple to say and hard to practice: **understand what people are working with before you design what they do.** Model the objects first. Then the pages, flows, and features have something coherent to stand on.

<Callout type="tip">

A fast way to find candidate objects: listen for the nouns users repeat when they describe their work. "I check a *student's* *scores* against the *standard* before I plan the *lesson*." Four likely objects in one sentence.

</Callout>
