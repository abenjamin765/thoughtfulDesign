## The situation

After the review that went nowhere, the designer tries again. This time she opens not with screens but with a sentence: "Today we need to decide whether the default dashboard view prioritizes *exceptions* the ops team must act on, or *totals* managers want to monitor. We can't optimize for both as the default."

The room changes immediately. The conversation has a center.

## What good framing looks like

A well-framed decision is **specific** and offers **real alternatives**. Compare:

| Unframed | Framed |
| --- | --- |
| "Let's talk about the dashboard." | "Should the default view prioritize exceptions or totals?" |
| "Get everyone's thoughts." | "Which of these two layouts better serves the ops team's daily triage?" |

Framing does three things: it tells people what to focus on, it reveals the real tradeoff, and it makes it obvious when the group has actually decided.

## Discussion is not decision

Groups slip easily into open discussion and mistake it for progress. Facilitation keeps the two distinct:

- **Discussion** explores options, surfaces information, and airs concerns.
- **Decision** commits to one option and names who owns the next step.

A good facilitator signals the transition out loud: "We've explored both — let's decide. Are we aligning on exceptions as the default?"

<Callout type="tip">

Use a "parking lot" for important points that are off-topic for today's decision. Capturing them visibly lets you honor the concern without losing the thread.

</Callout>

## Who is in the room

A decision needs two things present: the **information** to decide well and the **authority** to commit. Invite people who hold those. Notice who is *missing* — the team most affected by the choice often is not represented, and the decision suffers for it.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is inviting everyone with an opinion to avoid hurting feelings. Large rooms dilute responsibility and stall decisions. It is kinder and clearer to invite the right people and share the outcome with the rest.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="A framing checklist">

Before the meeting: What decision must we make? What are the real options? What tradeoff do they represent? Who has the information? Who has the authority? What will we walk out with?

</Reveal>
