## The situation

The dashboard decision is framed and the right people are present. Now the designer has to actually run the conversation — and conversations have a way of going soft, polite, and inconclusive unless someone shapes them.

## Better prompts

The questions you ask determine the feedback you get. Vague prompts get vague, taste-based answers. Specific, scenario-anchored prompts get useful ones.

| Weak prompt | Stronger prompt |
| --- | --- |
| "What do you think?" | "Where would the ops team get stuck during morning triage with this layout?" |
| "Any concerns?" | "What would have to be true for this default to fail the ops team?" |
| "Do you like the totals view?" | "When does a manager actually need totals before exceptions?" |

Good prompts point at a user, a moment, or a tradeoff — not at personal preference.

## Surfacing disagreement productively

Polite teams hide disagreement, and hidden disagreement does not disappear — it resurfaces after launch as "I never agreed to this." A facilitator's job is to make disagreement safe and explicit.

- Name it: "It sounds like sales wants totals up front and ops wants exceptions. That's a real tradeoff."
- Make the tradeoff visible: what each option gives up.
- Separate the people from the positions, so it is the ideas that compete, not the colleagues.

> Disagreement is not the problem in a meeting.
> Hidden disagreement is.

## Reaching and recording the decision

When the tradeoff is clear, move the group to decide — and then make the decision durable. A decision that lives only in people's memories will be relitigated next week.

Record three things:

1. **The decision** ("Default view prioritizes exceptions for the ops team.")
2. **The rationale** ("Ops uses the dashboard daily for triage; managers check totals weekly and can switch views.")
3. **The owner and next step** ("Maya updates the prototype by Friday.")

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is ending on a warm "sounds good, let's do it" without writing anything down. Warmth is not a decision record. If it is not captured with its rationale, expect to have the same meeting again.

</Callout>

## Try it

Use the **Facilitation Planner** resource. Plan a real upcoming meeting: its purpose, the decision needed, the participants (information + authority), two or three sharp prompts, the activities, and the expected output. Then run it.

<Reveal label="What good looks like">

A strong plan names a single specific decision, invites only the people who hold information or authority, includes prompts anchored to real user moments, and defines the concrete output (a recorded decision with an owner). After the meeting, the team can point to what was decided, why, and who is acting on it.

</Reveal>

## Summary

- Facilitation is a design skill: you design the conditions for good decisions, not just artifacts.
- Frame a specific decision with real alternatives before the meeting.
- Keep discussion and decision distinct, and signal the transition.
- Invite people who hold the information and authority the decision needs.
- Use specific, scenario-anchored prompts; surface disagreement instead of burying it.
- Record the decision, its rationale, and the owner so it sticks.
- This module is the **Align** step of the Thoughtful Design Loop.
