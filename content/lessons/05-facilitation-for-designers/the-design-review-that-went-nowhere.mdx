## The situation

A designer presents a redesign of an internal enterprise dashboard to a room of stakeholders. She walks through the screens. Then the feedback begins: sales wants a new widget, finance wants different totals, the engineering lead worries about load time, and someone asks why the chart isn't a different color.

Forty-five minutes later, she has twenty pieces of conflicting feedback and no idea what to do next. Everyone leaves feeling the meeting was "productive." Nothing was decided.

## The hidden problem

The meeting collected opinions because it never asked for a decision.

| What the meeting did | What it needed |
| --- | --- |
| Invited open reactions to screens | A specific decision to make |
| Let every stakeholder pull in a direction | A frame that focused the group |
| Mistook discussion for progress | A clear output everyone agreed to |

When a conversation has no decision at its center, people fill the vacuum with whatever they care about. The designer did not have a feedback problem. She had a framing problem.

> A meeting without a decision is a meeting that cannot end well.
> It can only end *eventually*.

## Facilitation is a design skill

A thoughtful designer designs more than screens. They design the conditions under which a group can make a good decision — what question is on the table, who is in the room, what gets discussed, and what the meeting must produce.

This does not require authority. It requires a few deliberate moves, starting with naming the decision out loud. The rest of this module gives you those moves.

<Callout type="tip">

Before any review, write one sentence: "By the end of this meeting, we will have decided ___." If you cannot fill the blank, the meeting is not ready to happen.

</Callout>
