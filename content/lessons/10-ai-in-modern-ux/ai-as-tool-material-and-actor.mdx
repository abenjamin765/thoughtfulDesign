## The situation

After the fabricated-quotes incident, the designer wants a clearer way to think about AI than "magic that's sometimes wrong." It helps to separate three roles AI plays in design work.

## Three lenses on AI

**AI as a tool** — something *you* use to do design work faster: synthesizing notes, drafting copy, generating ideas, modeling objects, running a first-pass critique. The output is an input to your judgment, never a replacement for it.

**AI as a design material** — something that appears *in the product* for users: a writing assistant, a recommendation, an autocomplete. Now AI is a material you design with, and it brings new design problems — how to show confidence, where answers came from, and how users recover when it is wrong.

**AI as a system actor** — something that *takes actions* inside the system: filtering, ranking, generating, deciding. Like any actor, it creates incentives and second-order effects (recall Module 3) and must be accounted for.

| Lens | Where AI lives | The key question |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Tool | In your workflow | Did I verify the output? |
| Material | In the user's interface | Does the user know how much to trust it? |
| Actor | In the system | What behavior and consequences does it create? |

## Prompting as structured thinking

Using AI well as a tool is less about clever phrasing and more about clear thinking. A good prompt names the goal, the context, the constraints, and the form of the answer — the same clarity you would give a capable colleague. Vague prompts get generic output; structured prompts get useful drafts. Prompting is thinking made explicit.

## Evaluating output critically

For any AI output you might rely on, check:

- **Grounding** — is it supported by real evidence or data, or invented?
- **Completeness** — what did it leave out, especially the inconvenient parts?
- **Bias** — whose perspective does it assume or erase?
- **Confidence vs. accuracy** — does its certain tone match its actual reliability?

> AI gives you a faster first draft.
> It does not give you a free pass on judgment.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is letting fluency stand in for verification. The more polished the output, the more carefully it deserves checking — because polish is exactly what makes errors slip through.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="Tool vs. material in one example">

In the AI writing assistant: as a *tool*, the designer uses AI to draft microcopy, then edits it. As a *material*, the product offers users AI-suggested sentences — so the team must design how suggestions show uncertainty, cite nothing falsely, and let users reject them easily. Same technology, two different design responsibilities.

</Reveal>
