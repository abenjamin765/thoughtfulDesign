## The situation

A designer on a team building an AI writing assistant is under deadline. She asks an AI tool to synthesize a stack of user interviews into key themes. In seconds it returns a clean, confident summary with five neat themes and supporting quotes. It looks like a week of work. She drops it straight into the deck.

In the review, someone who sat in the interviews frowns: two of the "quotes" were never said, and the biggest theme from the sessions is missing entirely.

## The hidden problem

The output was fluent, fast, and wrong in ways that fluency hid.

| What the AI delivered | What was actually true |
| --- | --- |
| Five tidy themes | The messiest, most important theme was omitted |
| Confident supporting quotes | Two quotes were fabricated |
| A polished, authoritative tone | No signal of uncertainty or gaps |

The danger was not that the AI helped. It was that its confidence and polish *substituted* for the designer's judgment. She trusted the surface and skipped the check.

> The AI did not make a mistake a human wouldn't.
> It made the mistake invisible by sounding sure.

## The core shift

AI can genuinely accelerate design work — research synthesis, modeling, ideation, critique, writing. But speed and polish are not truth. The thoughtful designer treats AI as a fast, fallible collaborator, not an authority. The judgment — what is true, what matters, what to do — stays with the human.

This is not anti-AI. It is the opposite of both blind trust and blanket refusal: use the tool, and keep the responsibility.

<Callout type="tip">

Treat AI output the way you'd treat a confident new intern's first draft: useful, fast, and absolutely requiring review before anyone relies on it.

</Callout>
