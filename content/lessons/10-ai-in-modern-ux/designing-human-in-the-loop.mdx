## The situation

The team's AI writing assistant is good but imperfect — it occasionally suggests confident, wrong, or biased text. The question is no longer whether to use AI, but how to design the feature so a human stays meaningfully in control.

## What human-in-the-loop means

Human-in-the-loop design keeps a person at the points where judgment matters, so the AI accelerates work without quietly taking over decisions it should not make. The skill is placing the checkpoint well — not everywhere (which defeats the speed) and not nowhere (which removes the safeguard).

Keep humans in the loop where an error would be **costly, hard to reverse, or harmful to others**. Let AI run more freely where mistakes are cheap and easy to undo.

| Lower stakes — AI can run | Higher stakes — keep a human checkpoint |
| --- | --- |
| Suggesting a phrasing the user can edit | Sending a message on the user's behalf |
| Drafting options to choose from | Making an irreversible or costly change |
| Surfacing a recommendation | Acting on a recommendation automatically |

## Designing for trust

For AI that reaches users, three things let people calibrate trust honestly:

- **Confidence** — communicate how sure the system is, without false precision.
- **Transparency / explainability** — show what the output is based on, so users can judge it.
- **Error recovery** — make it easy to notice a wrong output and undo or correct it.

A suggestion the user can preview, understand, and reject is trustworthy. One that acts silently and cannot be undone is not — no matter how good the model.

> The goal is not an AI that never errs.
> It is a design where the user can always tell when it did, and recover.

## Knowing when not to use AI

Part of judgment is restraint. Skip AI when the stakes are high and the model is unreliable, when it would obscure accountability, when it would exclude people (accessibility), or when a simpler, more transparent approach serves users better. Choosing *not* to add AI is a legitimate, often thoughtful, design decision.

## Try it

Use the **AI Judgment Checklist** resource. Take an AI-assisted workflow or feature and map: where AI helps, where it may mislead, what evidence is needed, what the human must decide, and how a user recovers from an error. Then place one clear human checkpoint and justify its location.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is automating the human checkpoint away under deadline pressure ("review is slowing us down"). The checkpoint is not friction to optimize out — it is the safeguard that makes the automation responsible.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="What good looks like">

A strong answer places the human checkpoint at the highest-stakes, least-reversible moment, and designs honest confidence, visible sourcing, and easy recovery around the AI's output. For the writing assistant: "AI drafts suggestions inline (low stakes), but never auto-sends. Suggestions show a confidence cue, can be expanded to see what they're based on, and are one tap to dismiss. The human always approves before anything leaves the editor."

</Reveal>

## Summary

- AI accelerates design work, but the designer keeps responsibility for judgment.
- Fluency and confidence are not accuracy; verify grounding, completeness, and bias.
- See AI through three lenses: tool (in your workflow), material (in the interface), actor (in the system).
- Prompting well is structured thinking: goal, context, constraints, form.
- Keep humans in the loop where errors are costly or irreversible; let AI run where mistakes are cheap.
- Design for confidence, transparency, and error recovery — and know when not to use AI.
- This module spans **Understand**, **Shape**, and **Account** in the loop.
