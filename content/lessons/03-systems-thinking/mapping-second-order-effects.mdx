## The situation

The school analytics team is about to ship a feature that automatically emails parents when a student is flagged at-risk. The first-order effect is obvious and good: parents get informed sooner. But the onboarding lesson taught them to look further before shipping.

## A simple tool: the consequence map

A **System Consequence Map** is a structured way to ask "what else moves?" For a proposed change, you trace its effects across the parts of the system that the screen does not show.

Map the change across at least these dimensions:

| Dimension | Question | Possible effect of auto-emailing parents |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Users | How does behavior change? | Some teachers stop flagging to avoid triggering emails |
| Support | What new load appears? | Parents reply with questions; someone must answer |
| Data | What gets distorted? | Under-flagging makes at-risk data less accurate |
| Business metrics | What looks better or worse? | "Parent engagement" rises; "flags" fall misleadingly |
| Trust | Who trusts the product less? | Teachers feel surveilled; parents may panic without context |
| Long-term behavior | What becomes normal? | Flagging becomes a high-stakes act instead of a quiet signal |

Suddenly a "simple notification" reveals a reinforcing loop that could corrupt the very data the platform depends on.

## From map to design move

A consequence map does not tell you to cancel the feature. It tells you where to design more carefully.

- Let teachers **add context** before a parent email goes out (keeps value, reduces panic).
- **Decouple** flagging from notifications so flagging stays a low-stakes signal (protects the data).
- Show teachers **who will be notified** before they flag (no surprises, preserves trust).

> A consequence map turns "ship and hope" into "anticipate and design."
> The goal is not to avoid all consequences — it is to choose them deliberately.

<Callout type="tip">

Run a consequence map on changes that alter incentives, automate a human judgment, or touch sensitive data. Those three categories produce the most surprising second-order effects.

</Callout>

## Try it

Use the **System Consequence Map** resource for this module. Take a real change you are considering and trace it across users, support, data, business metrics, trust, and long-term behavior. Then write one design move that keeps the benefit while reducing a harm you found.

<Callout type="warning">

A common trap is mapping consequences and then shipping unchanged anyway. The map only matters if it changes a decision. End every consequence map with at least one concrete design move.

</Callout>

<Reveal label="What good looks like">

A strong consequence map finds at least one non-obvious effect (something nobody raised in the kickoff), connects it to a feedback loop or incentive, and ends with a specific, buildable design change. "Auto-email could make teachers under-flag, so we will decouple flagging from notifications" is far stronger than "we should be careful about notifications."

</Reveal>

## Summary

- Every design decision changes behavior somewhere in the system.
- Systems are made of feedback loops, constraints, incentives, dependencies, and boundaries.
- Local optimization improves one metric while harming the whole; widen the boundary to see the global picture.
- A System Consequence Map anticipates second-order effects across users, support, data, metrics, trust, and time.
- This module touches the **Model** and **Account** steps of the Thoughtful Design Loop.

**Key terms:** system, feedback loop, constraint, incentive, dependency, system boundary, local vs. global optimization, second-order effect.
