## The situation

The school analytics team had one goal for the quarter: raise onboarding completion. Too many teachers signed up and never finished setup. So the team made onboarding shorter — they auto-skipped the steps where teachers connect their classes and import their rosters.

Completion shot up. The metric turned green. Everyone celebrated.

## The hidden problem

A month later, support tickets spiked. Teachers had "completed" onboarding but their dashboards were empty or wrong, because the skipped steps were the ones that actually made the product work. Some teachers concluded the tool was broken and stopped logging in.

| First-order effect (intended) | Second-order effects (unplanned) |
| --- | --- |
| Onboarding completion rose | Misconfigured accounts increased |
| Setup felt faster | Support tickets spiked |
| The metric looked healthy | Trust dropped; some teachers churned |

The team optimized one number and pushed the cost into a part of the system they were not looking at.

> They did not make onboarding better.
> They moved the difficulty from a place they measured to a place they didn't.

## The core idea

Every design decision changes behavior somewhere in the system. A product is not just a set of screens — it is a web of people, incentives, data, and feedback loops. When you press on one part, another part moves.

This is why a "win" on one metric can be a loss for the whole. Improving a single number while ignoring the system around it is called **local optimization**, and it is one of the most common ways thoughtful-looking decisions go wrong.

<Callout type="tip">

Before shipping a change that moves a metric, ask: "If this number goes up, what else might move — and would I see it?" The costs you cannot see are the ones that hurt most.

</Callout>
