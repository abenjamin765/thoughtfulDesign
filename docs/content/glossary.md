# Glossary — Thoughtful Design

> Shared term definitions. Every domain term gets a first-use definition in-lesson (typographic-pattern-spec §1.3). When a term appears for the first time in a lesson, define it inline or in a `Reveal`. This file is the canonical wording — keep lesson definitions consistent with it.

## Course-wide

- **Thoughtful design** — A practice of understanding the system behind a product before deciding what should appear on the screen, and accounting for the consequences of design decisions.
- **The Thoughtful Design Loop** — The course's recurring operating framework: Model → Understand → Align → Shape → Test → Account → Adapt.
- **System (UX sense)** — The people, objects, rules, incentives, data, and feedback loops that a product participates in — not just its interface.
- **Screen-level design** — Designing the visible surface (layouts, components, copy) of a single view.
- **System-level design** — Designing the objects, relationships, decisions, and consequences a product creates across people and time.

## Object-Oriented UX (Module 2)

- **Object** — A meaningful "thing" in a product's domain that users recognize and care about (e.g. Student, Appointment, Listing).
- **Attribute** — A property of an object (e.g. a Student's name, a Score's value).
- **Relationship** — A connection between objects (e.g. a Teacher *has* Classes).
- **Action / CTA** — Something a user can do to or with an object (e.g. assign, submit, archive).
- **State** — A condition an object can be in (e.g. an Assignment is draft, submitted, or graded).
- **Object map** — A low-fidelity model of a system's objects, their attributes, relationships, and actions.
- **Mental model** — How a user expects a system to be organized, based on prior experience.
- **SIP test** — A filter for whether a noun is a real object. In this course: **S**tructure (a recognizable shape users can point to), **I**dentity (instances are distinguishable), **P**urpose (it exists for a clear user-facing reason).
- **The Four Ancient Truths** — Objects (different things look different), Relationships (people navigate via relationships), CTAs (people act on objects directly), Attributes (same objects behave the same).
- **OOUX** — Object-Oriented UX. Unrelated to object-oriented programming.

> Note: the seed lessons used an alternate SIP expansion (Structure / Identity / Purpose). This is the canonical version for the course. Keep all lessons aligned to it.

## Systems thinking (Module 3)

- **Feedback loop** — When a system's output feeds back as input, reinforcing or balancing behavior.
- **Constraint** — A limit the system operates under (technical, organizational, legal, human).
- **Local optimization** — Improving one part or metric at the expense of the whole.
- **Second-order effect** — A downstream consequence of a change, beyond its intended first effect.
- **System boundary** — The line a designer draws around what is "in" the system being designed.

## Research (Module 4)

- **Research question** — A question the *team* needs answered to make a decision (not a question you ask a participant).
- **Interview question** — A question you actually ask a participant; should be non-leading.
- **Assumption** — A belief the team holds without direct evidence.
- **Evidence strength** — How much confidence a finding can bear (observed > borrowed > assumed).
- **Synthesis** — Turning raw observations into insights that inform decisions.

## Facilitation & strategy (Modules 5–6)

- **Facilitation** — Creating the conditions for a group to make a better decision.
- **Decision framing** — Stating clearly what decision a conversation must produce.
- **Tradeoff** — Giving up some value on one dimension to gain value on another.
- **Design rationale** — The reasoning that connects a recommendation to user needs, goals, evidence, and tradeoffs.
- **Satisficing** — Choosing a good-enough option under real constraints rather than searching for the theoretical best.

## Craft, accessibility, ethics (Modules 7–9)

- **Cognitive load** — The mental effort a task demands of a user.
- **Progressive disclosure** — Revealing complexity only as the user needs it.
- **Recognition vs. recall** — Letting users recognize options rather than remember them.
- **Human variation** — The full range of human abilities and situations: permanent, temporary, and situational.
- **WCAG** — Web Content Accessibility Guidelines; the course teaches WCAG-informed thinking, not compliance checklists alone.
- **Situational disability** — A temporary limitation caused by context (bright sun, one hand busy, noisy room).
- **Dark pattern** — A design that manipulates users into actions against their interest.
- **Persuasion vs. manipulation** — Persuasion respects the user's ability to choose; manipulation undermines it.
- **Power asymmetry** — When the product holds far more information or control than the user.

## AI & practice (Modules 10–11)

- **Human-in-the-loop** — A design where a person reviews or approves an AI action before it takes effect.
- **Hallucination** — When an AI produces confident output that is false or unsupported.
- **Explainability** — How well a system can show *why* it produced an output.
- **Confidence (AI)** — A signal of how certain the system is; must be communicated honestly.
- **Decision record** — A short, durable note capturing a decision, its rationale, and its tradeoffs.
- **Reflective practice** — Regularly examining your own decisions to improve judgment over time.
